BAUHAUS

BAUHAUS

1919

The Bauhaus was a groundbreaking design school founded in 1919 in Weimar, Germany, by architect Walter Gropius. It sought to unify art, craft, and technology, promoting the idea that design should serve society and be functional. Rejecting decorative excess, the school embraced simplicity, geometric forms, and clean lines, championing the principle that "form follows function." The Bauhaus movement significantly influenced architecture, graphic design, product design, and typography. Its faculty and students—including Paul Klee, Wassily Kandinsky, and Marcel Breuer—emphasized experimentation and interdisciplinary collaboration. Iconic Bauhaus works often feature modular structures, sans-serif typography, and a minimalist aesthetic. Although the school was closed by the Nazis in 1933, its legacy continued to spread globally as key figures emigrated. Today, Bauhaus principles remain deeply embedded in contemporary design practices across disciplines.

Bauhaus has been a lasting source of inspiration in my design journey. In this course, its influence became particularly evident in the layout of my website. For instance, in Workbook 2C, I incorporated color-coded buttons for navigation between pages—a subtle nod to Bauhaus ideals. I’m especially drawn to designs that use minimal structures with strategic use of color to convey personality and function, reflecting the timeless appeal of the Bauhaus philosophy.

DANIEL WENZEL

DANIEL WENZEL

2019

Daniel Wenzel's Automated Type Design project was developed in 2019 as part of his thesis at HTWG Konstanz in Germany. The project investigates the intersection of automation, artificial intelligence, and type design, resulting in the creation of over 100 typefaces within a 16-week period. The accompanying website serves as an interactive showcase of his thesis and was launched in conjunction with the project’s exhibition at the Werkschau HTWG Konstanz during the summer of 2019. The site outlines Wenzel’s five-step framework for automating type design—Form, Systematics, Extension, Refinement, and Variation—and features numerous examples of typefaces generated using both systematic and AI-driven processes. This work demonstrates Wenzel’s innovative approach to integrating technology with creative practice and contributes meaningfully to the evolving conversation around automation in design..

What stands out to me is his effective incorporation of experimental graphics throughout the website, which highlights the depth of his process. By integrating advanced technology into his design methodology, Wenzel successfully elevates the final visual outcomes. One particularly striking example involves a large-scale digital installation composed of nine interconnected screens forming a cohesive visual piece. As someone who appreciates a monochromatic color palette, I was especially drawn to his ability to achieve contrast, texture, and tonal variation using minimal color. The animated components of the website were also a source of inspiration for me — elements I drew from and applied in my own work during Weeks 7 and 8 of this course.

PULSE ROOM

PULSE ROOM

2021

Pulse Room is an interactive installation by artist Rafael Lozano-Hemmer that transforms biometric data into a mesmerizing visual experience. The artwork features a room filled with hundreds of incandescent light bulbs, each flashing in sync with the heartbeat of a participant. When a viewer places their hand on a sensor, their pulse is detected and translated into a rhythm of light, which is then added to the sequence of previous participants’ pulses. This piece explores the relationship between technology, human presence, and collective memory. The simplicity of the visual concept—light blinking to the rhythm of a heartbeat—makes the experience deeply personal and emotionally resonant. I find Pulse Room inspiring for digital design because of how it merges data, interactivity, and minimalism into something poetic. It reminds me that design can be both functional and emotionally driven, using technology not just for utility but for creating immersive, human-centered experiences.

STUDIO ONGARATO

STUDIO ONGARATO

1993

Studio Ongarato is a Melbourne-based, internationally recognised design studio renowned for its innovative approach to branding, wayfinding, placemaking, and experiential environments. Established in 1993 by Creative Director Fabio Ongarato and Director Ronnen Goren, the studio has consistently challenged conventional design norms, crafting culturally influential brands and experiences over the past three decades. With offices in Melbourne, Hong Kong, and the Americas, Studio Ongarato operates across diverse sectors, including hospitality, property, arts, and culture. Their multidisciplinary team comprises designers, curators, editors, architects, art directors, and craftspeople, collaborating closely with leading artists and artisans to deliver unique and compelling outcomes for each project. Notable projects include branding and environmental graphics for the Jackalope Hotel, wayfinding systems for the Western Australian Museum, and identity design for the National Gallery of Australia. The studio's work is characterised by a rigorous, strategic interrogation of brand and context, aiming to connect on an experiential level that elevates clients to the forefront of cultural influence.

Studio Ongorato has made outstanding projects that I take great inspiration from. I chose Studio Ongarato as inspiration because of their bold, conceptual approach to design that seamlessly blends storytelling, spatial experience, and visual identity. Their work pushes boundaries and engages audiences emotionally, which aligns with my own goals to create meaningful, immersive design outcomes that go beyond conventional aesthetics.
